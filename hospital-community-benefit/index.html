When a hospital is acquired, does it give more to charity?

To keep their untaxed status, nonprofit hospitals must give an amount of money known as a “community benefit.” When Ingalls Memorial Hospital was acquired by UChicago Medicine, its charity giving did not see a long-term increase.

By Jade-Ruyu Yan

Ingalls Memorial Hospital, which serves Harvey, IL, was bought by The University of Chicago Medicine in 2016 after announcing a plan the year before to unite with a larger entity to achieve greater financial stability.

But an analysis of almost a decade’s worth of Illinois hospital’s 990 forms indicates that this acquisition does not appear to have come with an increase in charitable giving.


Community benefit encompasses giving financial aid to low-income patients and generally improving community health, according to the Lown Institute, a think tank that studies and advocates for equitable healthcare.

Although hospitals are required to spend some amount on “community benefit” to keep their nonprofit status, there is no minimum amount that hospitals are required to give as their community benefit.

Hospitals used this lack of rules around spending to spend very little, according to studies cited in an explainer from the organisation.

Data show that although UChicago’s Ingalls Memorial net giving increased by almost 30%, or almost $20 million, in the year that UChicago acquired the hospital, there was no long-term increase afterwards. The hospital's net community benefit giving fell to numbers that were similar to or lower than its giving prior to the acquisition.



Ingalls has spent the majority of its community benefit on Medicaid over the past decade, with comparatively little going to community health initiatives.

Certain types of payments—including Medicaid and training of health professionals—have been criticized by Lown Institute as not providing meaningful benefit to the community.
